Sigiriya Rock Fortress

Ancient Engineering Marvel · Lion Rock · UNESCO World Heritage Site

View Gallery Learn More

Built: 5th Century AD (477-495)

Height: 200 meters (660 ft)

UNESCO: Since 1982

Built by: King Kasyapa I

The Eighth Wonder of the World

Sigiriya, meaning "Lion Rock," is an ancient rock fortress located in the central Matale District of Sri Lanka. This 5th-century masterpiece is one of the best-preserved examples of ancient urban planning.

Built by King Kasyapa (477-495 AD), Sigiriya is renowned for its sophisticated city planning, magnificent frescoes, advanced hydraulic systems, and stunning Mirror Wall inscriptions. The rock rises dramatically 200 meters above the surrounding plain and is visible for miles in every direction.

The site combines ancient engineering with artistic genius. Water gardens, boulder gardens, and terraced gardens spread across the western face, while the famous frescoes and the Mirror Wall grace the western cliff face. The massive Lion's Gate at the entrance once featured a gigantic lion structure, from which the fortress takes its name.

Historical Background

The Legend of King Kasyapa

According to the Culavamsa chronicle, Prince Kasyapa overthrew his father King Dhatusena and took the throne. Fearing revenge from his half-brother Moggallana (the rightful heir), Kasyapa built this impregnable fortress atop Sigiriya rock. He reigned from 477 to 495 AD.

The Royal Citadel

During Kasyapa's 18-year reign, Sigiriya was transformed into a magnificent palace complex with gardens, pools, and fountains. The upper palace on the rock summit was accessible only through the Lion's Gate.

After Kasyapa

After Kasyapa's defeat in 495 AD, Sigiriya became a Buddhist monastery until the 14th century. The site was then abandoned and forgotten until British explorers rediscovered it in the 19th century.

Key Attractions

Sigiriya Frescoes

Ancient paintings of celestial maidens (Apsaras) on the western cliff face. Originally 500 frescoes, only 22 survive today.

Mirror Wall

Highly polished wall with ancient graffiti dating from 8th-10th centuries. Over 1,800 inscriptions in Sinhala poetry.

Lion's Gate

Massive lion paws carved from rock. Visitors once entered through the lion's mouth to reach the upper palace.

Water Gardens

Symmetrical gardens with sophisticated hydraulic systems, pools, and fountains that still function during rainy season.

Summit Palace

Royal palace ruins on the 1.6-hectare summit with stunning 360° panoramic views of the surrounding jungle.

Boulder Gardens

Natural boulder formations incorporated into architectural design with caves used as meditation chambers.

Ancient Engineering

Sigiriya demonstrates remarkable engineering achievements:

  • Hydraulic Technology: Advanced water pressure systems powered fountains and pools throughout the complex
  • Urban Planning: Symmetrical city layout with eastern and western precincts divided by a central axis
  • Climate Control: Natural air conditioning through rock tunnels and water features
  • Defensive Architecture: Multiple terraces and defensive structures made the fortress nearly impregnable
